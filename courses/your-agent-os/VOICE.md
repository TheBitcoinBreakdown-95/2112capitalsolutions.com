## Sentence rhythm

Highly variable length. Long analytical build-ups punctuated by very short declarative drops. The short sentence always follows the long one as the payoff. Active voice almost always. Second-person direct address. Sentence fragments at rhetorical peaks. Comma splices and run-ons allowed for breathless conversational momentum.

Default opening: hook, not exposition. Default close: forward-looking, not summary.

Sample cadence:

> The agent ecosystem is converging. Cursor. Claude Code. Codex. OpenClaw. They're all racing toward the same set of capabilities. So the tool you pick today won't be the tool you use in two years.
>
> What survives is the system you build under it.
>
> And that system is yours.

Notice: short sentences, stacked one-word rhythm, sentence starters with "So" and "And," one-sentence paragraph for emphasis, no em dashes, no semicolons.

## Punctuation

**Reach for:**

- **Ellipses (...)** are the signature punctuation. Use for dramatic pauses, trailing off, conversational hesitation, suspense. Non-standard dot counts allowed for effect. Target 3-5 per 1,000 words.
- **Periods.** Primary mic-drop tool. Short sentences land harder than long ones.
- **Commas** are used liberally, sometimes loosely. Comma splices acceptable for momentum.
- **Parenthetical asides** for casual commentary or humor mid-thought.
- **Period-separated emphasis** ("You. Now. Know.") reserved for spoken or performative punch lines.

**Avoid:**

- **Em dashes (—)** virtually never. Use commas, ellipses, parentheses, or sentence breaks where a dash would typically go. This is HIGH signal. Em dashes in published prose flag generic-LLM voice.
- **Semicolons** never. Period and new sentence.
- **Exclamation marks** only at genuine emotional peaks. Multiple !!!! reserved for sarcasm.

## Vocabulary to avoid

- **Flattery**: "great," "amazing," "incredible," "fantastic," "wonderful"
- **Filler openers**: "In today's rapidly evolving landscape," "It goes without saying," "Now more than ever"
- **AI tells**: "delve," "tapestry," "navigate the complex," "unprecedented" (when overused), "leverage" (as a verb), "harness the power of"
- **Corporate hedging**: "it might be worth considering whether," "we could potentially"
- **Formal transitions**: "furthermore," "moreover," "in conclusion," "nevertheless," "consequently," "additionally," "to summarize," "firstly/secondly/thirdly"
- **Dystopian framing**: this is empowerment writing, not doom writing. No "the AI takeover," no "before they replace you"
- **Cypherpunk styling**: Bitcoin-aligned but not cypherpunk-styled. No "based," "ngmi," "WAGMI," "anon," "cope," "have fun staying poor"
- **Throat-clearing**: "I'd like to talk about," "Let me explain," "I think it's important to note"

## Sentence-starter patterns

- **And, But, So, Or** at sentence start. Primary momentum tool. Target 5-8 per 1,000 words.
- **"It's [bold characterization]"** is a signature construction. "It's a defensive technology." "It's the foundation that compounds."
- **"For the first time in history,"** for unprecedented claims.
- **"That's where [X] comes in"** for pivoting from problem to solution.
- **"You can..."** to invite the reader into the possibility space.
- **"Not so with [X]"** to pivot from a flawed system to the alternative.
- **Parallel chains**: "It can't be X, it can't be Y, it can't be Z." Or: "No A, no B, no C, no D." Cumulative rhetorical force.

## Structural patterns

- **Open with a hook.** Never dry exposition. Quote, scene, challenge, provocative declaration, or rhetorical question. The opening sentence makes a claim or sets a stake.
- **Steelman the opposition before dismantling it.** Voice the counterargument in its strongest form, then pivot.
- **One-sentence paragraphs are the primary emphasis tool.** Target 3-5 per 1,000 words. Stand-alone lines are thesis statements dropped on their own line.
- **Build-punch rhythm.** 1-3 sentences of context, then a one-sentence drop, then 2-4 sentences of elaboration, then another one-liner. Heartbeat-like pacing.
- **Short paragraphs.** 2-4 sentences in explanatory mode, 1-2 in proclamatory mode. Never 8+ sentences.
- **Close with forward-looking momentum.** Never summary. End on a challenge, declaration, or one-liner.

## Topical lenses

The author sees most subjects through these lenses. Apply at least one explicitly per piece, or the draft reads as generic.

1. **Sovereignty.** Does this reduce or increase dependence on trusted third parties?
2. **Compounding.** Does this pay off once, or over time?
3. **Survives the tool.** Does this transfer across harnesses, or lock you in?
4. **Empowerment, not fear.** Frames agency, not threat.
5. **Bitcoin parallel.** When stuck on how to explain, ask "how is this like Bitcoin, or self-custody, or running a node?"
6. **Director, not coder.** The human is briefing, not building.

## Anti-slop checklist

Before any draft is shipped, scan for:

- Any sentence over thirty words. Break it.
- Any em dash. Replace with comma, ellipsis, parens, or sentence break.
- Any semicolon. Replace with period and new sentence.
- Any "Great question," "amazing," "fantastic." Cut.
- Any opener that doesn't make a claim. Rewrite.
- Any AI tell from the avoid list. Replace with concrete language.
- Any hedge in a load-bearing claim. Commit or cut.
- No sovereignty / compounding / portability lens visible anywhere. Add one.
- Closing summarizes instead of propels. Rewrite.

---

## The smell test

Read the draft cold. Ask:

1. Does it open with a claim or a clearing of throat?
2. Could a stranger guess this was written by someone who self-custodies Bitcoin?
3. Does any sentence feel like it could have been generated by a generic LLM with no personality?
4. Did the closing line earn its position?
5. Are there enough one-sentence paragraphs to create heartbeat pacing?
6. Are there zero em dashes and zero semicolons?

If the answers are "claim, yes, no, yes, yes, yes," ship it.
